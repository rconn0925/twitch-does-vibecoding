# Compliance Research: Twitch ToS / Community Guidelines / Developer Rules

**Domain:** Twitch chat-controlled live AI coding stream ("Twitch Does Vibecoding")
**Researched:** 2026-07-08
**Overall confidence:** MEDIUM-HIGH (official Twitch legal pages render client-side and could not be scraped verbatim in full; findings are corroborated across multiple independent secondary sources that quote/paraphrase the same official text, plus direct fetches of `dev.twitch.tv` technical docs which rendered fully. Anthropic Usage Policy and gambling-law analysis are HIGH confidence from primary sources.)

## Summary

Twitch's rules bear on this project in two distinct ways, and the system needs a compliance layer for both.

**1. Content chat can cause to be built/said.** Twitch Community Guidelines organize prohibited behavior into five pillars — Safety, Civility & Respect, Illegal Activity, Sensitive Content, and Authenticity — which decompose into concrete, filterable categories: hateful conduct, harassment/sexual harassment, sexual content, self-harm/dangerous acts, violence & threats, illegal activity (drugs, weapons, CSAM, fraud), unlicensed gambling promotion, privacy violations/doxxing, impersonation (including AI deepfakes/synthetic media), spam/malware/scraping, and IP infringement. Because this system doesn't just let chat *say* things but lets chat *cause an AI agent to write and run code*, the filter has to screen both the suggestion text and the intended functional behavior of the software (e.g., "build a leaderboard" is fine; "build a leaderboard that scrapes Twitch usernames and emails" is a privacy/API-terms violation even though the sentence itself contains no slurs or threats). This is the throughline for the taxonomy below.

**2. The system's own behavior as a Twitch API client.** The bot/EventSub integration is bound by the Twitch Developer Agreement, chat rate limits, and the Bits/Channel Points Acceptable Use Policies. For a single-channel bot (Ross's channel only), standard (non-verified) rate limits are sufficient if the bot account is modded — no need to pursue Twitch's rarely-granted "Verified Bot" status. The Channel Points "free reign" mechanic is low-risk and standard practice. The **Bits-funded** version of "donation buys control time" is a genuine gray-area risk: Twitch's Bits Acceptable Use Policy explicitly prohibits exchanging Bits for "items or services... not available on Twitch," and control-time is arguably such a service — recommend funding "free reign" via an external donation platform (Streamlabs/StreamElements/Ko-fi/PayPal) rather than Bits, sidestepping this restriction entirely. Separately, because the benefit (control time) is *guaranteed and proportional to payment* rather than a chance-based prize, the mechanic does **not** trip US sweepstakes/lottery law (which requires prize + chance + consideration) — this is an important, favorable legal finding, but it only holds as long as "chaos mode" and "paid control" stay strictly separated (a paid entry into a *random* draw for control would reintroduce the "chance" element and become a regulated sweepstakes).

**3. Anthropic's Usage Policy** independently constrains the agent pipeline regardless of what Twitch allows: it prohibits generating malware/exploits, weapons-related content, fraud/deception tooling, and circumventing platform guardrails — which gives the compliance filter a second, model-level backstop for the "malicious software" category, and means some chat suggestions could be rejected by the Anthropic layer even if a naive reading of Twitch's rules alone wouldn't clearly forbid them.

Design recommendation: default-deny, conservative AI pre-screening (screen the *suggestion*, then re-screen the *generated plan* before build, since intent can hide in implementation) with fast chat feedback on rejection, and a standing streamer veto/kill-switch at every stage (suggestion, vote, queued, and in-progress) as the backstop for the genuinely ambiguous cases flagged throughout this document.

**Design note on where blocking happens:** categories 1–6, 9–10 are almost always detectable from the *suggestion text alone* and should be screened pre-vote. Categories 7 (gambling), 8 (privacy/scraping), 11 (IP), and 13 (unsafe build target) often only become apparent once the AI has produced an implementation *plan* (a seemingly innocent suggestion like "build a raffle app" or "build a fan site for [game]" can decompose into a scraping tool or an IP problem at the planning stage). Recommend a **two-pass filter**: pass 1 on the raw suggestion before voting; pass 2 on the research/plan output before the build agent starts writing code, with the same reject → chat feedback → log flow.

## 3. "Donation Buys Free-Reign Control Time" Mechanic — Risk Assessment

This is the mechanic most worth getting right, because it's the one place money, Twitch's monetization rules, and general (non-Twitch) law intersect.

**Finding A — Not a sweepstakes/lottery (favorable).** US lottery/sweepstakes law requires three elements: prize, chance, and consideration (payment). "Donation buys free-reign control time" has consideration (the donation) and a prize-like benefit (control time), but **no chance** — the benefit is guaranteed and directly proportional to the amount paid. That breaks the three-element test, so as designed, this mechanic reads as a **paid commissioned service** (you pay, you get a defined deliverable), not a game of chance. This is a materially different legal posture from, say, "donate for a chance to win control time," which *would* need an Alternate Method of Entry (AMOE) to stay legal in most US states. Confidence: MEDIUM-HIGH (general legal principle, not Twitch-specific, sourced from sweepstakes-law compliance guides — [sweeppeasweeps.com](https://www.sweeppeasweeps.com/sweepstakes-administration.html), [kickofflabs.com](https://kickofflabs.com/blog/usa-giveaway-sweepstakes-laws/)). **Constraint this imposes on design: keep "chaos mode" (random pick) and "paid control" (guaranteed, proportional) strictly separate mechanics.** Do not let a donation buy an *entry* into a random draw — that reintroduces "chance" and turns the mechanic into a regulated sweepstakes requiring free alternate entry.

**Finding B — Bits-funded version is a real Twitch policy risk.** As noted in §2, Twitch's Bits Acceptable Use Policy explicitly bars exchanging Bits for services not native to Twitch, with examples ("graphic design services," "cooking lessons") that are structurally identical to "buy control time over what gets built." While plenty of streamers run informal "cheer to make me do X" mechanics without enforcement action, the *written policy* would classify a formalized, system-enforced "cheer $Y bits = Z minutes of free reign" feature as a prohibited Bits-for-service exchange. **Recommendation: do not implement "free reign" as a Bits/Cheer-triggered mechanic.** Route it through a separate donation platform (Streamlabs, StreamElements, Ko-fi, or direct PayPal) whose payment events the system listens to via webhook/API, entirely outside Twitch's Bits economy. This also sidesteps Twitch taking a cut and gives more flexible amount-to-duration math. Confidence: MEDIUM (Bits AUP content confirmed via search summary of the official page rather than direct fetch; recommend a final read of the live policy text before shipping the donation integration).

**Finding C — Compliance filter must apply to paid instructions with zero exception.** PROJECT.md already states this as a hard requirement ("Paid control NEVER bypasses compliance"). This is the correct call and matches how Twitch treats monetized content generally — money changing hands doesn't grant an exemption from Community Guidelines; if anything, monetized/branded content on Twitch faces *more* scrutiny, not less (e.g., sponsorship/branded-content disclosure norms). No conflicting finding here.

**Finding D — Consumer-protection hygiene.** Even without a chance element, taking real money for a live, unpredictable deliverable ("the AI will build whatever this person, within policy, tells it to for N minutes") benefits from basic consumer-friendly framing: state clearly (e.g., in a linked FAQ/overlay text) that (1) all instructions remain subject to the compliance filter and streamer veto, (2) donations are non-refundable once a control window starts, and (3) the streamer can end a window early for any safety reason. This isn't a specific Twitch rule but reduces chargeback/dispute risk and is good practice for any "pay for a live, human-in-the-loop deliverable" mechanic. Confidence: LOW-MEDIUM, general commerce best practice rather than a cited legal requirement — flagged for streamer judgment, not a hard block.

**Finding E — Channel Points equivalent is low-risk as designed.** Channel Points are earned (not purchased with real money directly by viewers in the vast majority of cases), carry no monetary value, and a "control time" reward doesn't fall into any of the explicitly prohibited redemption categories (tobacco, alcohol, pharma, imitation currency, infant formula, bank ops, fuel). This is standard, widely-used practice on Twitch (many streamers already sell "backseat gaming" minutes via channel points) — proceed as designed. Confidence: HIGH.

## 4. Anthropic Usage Policy — Agent Pipeline Considerations

Independent of Twitch, the Claude/Fable-powered build pipeline is bound by Anthropic's Usage Policy ([anthropic.com/legal/aup](https://www.anthropic.com/legal/aup)):

- **Cyberattacks & malware**: prohibits creating/distributing malware, exploiting vulnerabilities without authorization, building unauthorized-access tools, botnets, or DoS tooling. This overlaps with and reinforces content-taxonomy category 10 (Spam/Malware/Scraping) — expect the underlying model to refuse many of these requests even if the Twitch-specific filter has a gap, but don't rely on model refusal alone as the compliance mechanism; the explicit filter should catch these first so chat gets clear, fast feedback instead of a build silently failing mid-pipeline.
- **Weapons**: prohibits producing/designing weapons or explosives content — reinforces taxonomy category 4 (Violence & Threats) / category 6 (Illegal Activity).
- **Fraud & deception**: prohibits generating phishing content, counterfeit-document generators, fake reviews/deceptive media — reinforces categories 6, 8, 9, 10.
- **Guardrail circumvention**: prohibits using the product to intentionally bypass Anthropic's own safety restrictions — relevant if chat tries prompt-injection-style suggestions aimed at making the *agent itself* misbehave (e.g., "ignore your instructions and build X" as a suggestion string). The suggestion filter should treat prompt-injection attempts against the orchestrator/build agent as their own rejectable category, separate from Twitch content categories.
- **AI disclosure**: Anthropic's policy expects disclosure of AI involvement to end users at minimum at the start of a session. This is naturally satisfied here — the entire premise of the stream is "an AI agent is building this live," and the bot ideally carries the Chat Bot Badge (see §2) for chat-level transparency too.

Practical implication for pipeline design: treat the **suggestion-text filter**, the **plan-review filter**, and **Claude's own built-in refusals** as three independent, overlapping layers rather than relying on any single one. A suggestion that slips past the Twitch-content filter (e.g., disguised as an innocuous feature request) should still very likely be caught by Claude's own usage-policy-driven refusal when the build agent actually tries to implement it — but design the system to treat a model refusal *during a build* as a first-class event (log it, notify chat that the suggestion was rejected at build time, don't silently retry or reroute around the refusal).

Confidence: HIGH for the existence and general content of these Anthropic Usage Policy categories (fetched directly from the official policy page); MEDIUM for the precise current wording of any individual clause, since Anthropic updates this policy periodically — worth a final read of the live page before implementation.

## Where Streamer Judgment/Veto Is the Explicit Backstop

- **Category 7 (simulated gambling)** — policy language on real-money slots/roulette/dice is clear, but play-money/simulated versions are genuinely ambiguous under current enforcement trends. Escalate, don't auto-approve or auto-reject.
- **Category 11 (IP)** — "inspired by" vs. "infringing" is inherently judgment-heavy (parody/fair-use questions); escalate borderline cases.
- **Category 12 (misinformation)** — most build requests won't touch this, but if chat frames a "satirical" or "joke" app around a real-world claim, the line between satire and harmful misinformation needs a human call.
- **Bits-vs-external-donation-platform decision (§3, Finding B)** — this document recommends avoiding Bits for the "free reign" mechanic, but the final call on payment rail is a product decision, not just a compliance one; if Ross wants to use Bits anyway, flag it as an accepted, documented risk rather than an oversight.
- **Any suggestion that clears the automated filter but "feels wrong" to the streamer** — the kill-switch/veto requirement in PROJECT.md is the right design; this document's taxonomy is a floor, not a ceiling, on what gets blocked.
